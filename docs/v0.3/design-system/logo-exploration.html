Terminal Talk — logo exploration

Five directions, each shown at four sizes plus three real contexts. No abstract glyphs, no "corporate SaaS" vibes. Each mark earns its square by being honest to what the product is: a floating pill with one coloured dot at a time.

5 directions
4 sizes each
3 contexts each
A
The Dot
The product's identity is literally a coloured dot. This mark is exactly that — one saturated green circle with the signature pulsing halo (the "active" state). No metaphor, no trickery. The product is the logo.
Trade-off Bold. Scales to 16px perfectly. But "just a dot" reads as incomplete to some — you have to commit.

B
The Pill
A frozen micro-toolbar. The pill shape IS the brand — four dots in different session colours, rendered as an app icon. Anyone who's used Terminal Talk recognises this instantly: it's the product, miniaturised.
Trade-off Most product-accurate. But the horizontal pill shape fights the square canvas of an app icon — needs padding above/below, which wastes pixels at 16px.

C
TT monogram
Two Ts built from terminal box-drawing characters. Classic monogram, but every stroke is a nod to ASCII. The crossbar of the second T is replaced with a coloured dot — smuggling the product identity into the mark itself.
Trade-off Most "logo-ish" and probably most versatile. But a pure monogram is less daring than the dot or pill options.

D
The Caret
A terminal prompt ❯ in session-colour green, with a quiet dot sitting on the baseline where the cursor would blink. "The thing that talks" = the prompt. Feels native to the terminal world.
Trade-off Terminal-heritage at its strongest. Carets are a little clichéd in dev tools (Warp, Ghostty) — the dot below is what separates it.

E
Dot + Waveform
A coloured dot with three concentric arcs emanating to one side — colour is the source, voice is what it emits. Tells the whole product story in one symbol: "this dot speaks."
Trade-off Most literal / "explains itself" at first glance. But it borrows the routing-signal visual that's used by WiFi, AirPlay, etc. — it's communicating something specific, at the cost of being a touch generic.

B — The Pill

The product is a floating pill with coloured dots. Making the pill itself the icon is the most honest, most memorable move. At 1024px it's a beautiful object; at 16px the pill outline + one dot still reads. It's also the only option that scales up into a wordmark lockup naturally — the mark becomes the wordmark:
